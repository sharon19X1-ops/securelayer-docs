SecureLayer

AI Agent Security Management — Business Model Validation
Office Hours Session Findings

Date: May 25, 2026 Method: YC Office Hours — 5 Forcing Questions (Startup Mode) Target market: MSSPs & SMBs

Product Context

SecureLayer is an integrated security management layer designed for MSSPs (Managed Security Service Providers) and SMBs. It manages AI agents — Claude Code, GitHub Copilot, Cursor, MCP servers, and custom connectors — running on developer endpoints, providing office hours enforcement, skill scanning, policy enforcement, multi-tenant visibility, and integration with PSA/SIEM/RMM platforms.

This session applied the YC Office Hours methodology to stress-test the business model before any sales or go-to-market investment. The goal: distinguish validated demand from inferred demand, and identify the narrowest viable wedge for monetization.

Session Transcript — The Five Forcing Questions

Q1 · Demand Reality
What's the strongest evidence you have that an MSSP or SMB would actually pay for SecureLayer — not "sounds interesting," not "we should look into this," but would be genuinely upset if it disappeared tomorrow?
Answer
"The demand is inferred — AI agent sprawl is growing, someone must need this."
Facilitator note: This is the most common early-stage failure pattern — solution in search of a problem. A growing market does not equal paying customers. Buyers act on pain they feel today, not trends they read about. No validated demand has been established yet. This is the most important gap to close before any pricing or GTM investment.
Q2 · Status Quo
What are MSSPs or SMBs doing right now to manage AI agents on their endpoints — even badly? What does that workaround cost them?
Answer
"They have no process at all — AI agents run unmanaged and nobody's tracking them."
Facilitator note: "No process" is a double-edged finding. It confirms a genuine gap — but it also means there is no burning urgency yet. If nobody is being held accountable for an AI agent incident, there is no trigger to buy. The key question this raises: who gets fired when an AI agent causes a breach? If the answer is unclear, the buying urgency is also unclear. This gap may close rapidly as AI agents proliferate and the first high-profile MSSP incident occurs.
Q3 · Desperate Specificity
Name the actual human who needs SecureLayer most. Not "MSSPs" or "SMBs" — a title, a role, a specific consequence they face. What keeps them up at night? What gets them fired?
Answer
"MSSP vCISO / Security Director — responsible for client security posture across 50–500 SMBs, gets blamed when a client has an incident."
Facilitator note: This is the sharpest answer in the session. A vCISO who manages 50–500 clients has a scalability problem — they cannot personally monitor AI agent behavior across all clients. SecureLayer directly extends their capacity. The "gets blamed" framing is the right career-consequence framing for a B2B security pitch. This persona should anchor all sales conversations.
Q4 · Narrowest Wedge
What's the smallest version of SecureLayer that an MSSP vCISO would pay for this week — before you build anything else? One thing. Something deliverable in days, not months.
Answer
"A combination: a visibility report showing all AI agents running across client environments, plus a risk score per tenant/client that can justify the use and cost of the service."
Facilitator note: The key insight here is that the risk score is doing the most strategic work — it is not just a feature, it is the invoice justification. An MSSP vCISO can show their CFO or client: "Our AI agent risk score across managed clients dropped from 7.2 to 2.1 since deployment." That is a renewal conversation and an upsell conversation in one number. The wedge answer was pushed to a combination; the sharper single wedge is the risk scorecard. Visibility and control are the retained product. The risk score is the business case.
Q5 · Observation & Surprise
Have you sat with an MSSP vCISO and watched how they manage client security today — without guiding them, without pitching? What surprised you?
Answer
"No — but I have close relationships and strong intuition from adjacent experience."
Facilitator note: Adjacent experience and relationships are real assets — but they are not observation. The gap between intuition and direct observation is where most business model assumptions collapse. Specifically, what is unknown: whether the vCISO already has a workaround (a real competitor), whether they perceive AI agent security as a priority category at all, and whether they would consume a risk report or ignore it. This is the single largest unvalidated risk in the model.

Business Model Recommendation

Recommended Model

MSSP-channel, per-tenant SaaS with a risk scorecard wedge.

Charge the MSSP $20–30/tenant/month. They mark it up to $50–60/tenant/month and present it to SMB clients as "AI Security Monitoring." At 80 tenants, this is $1,600–2,400/month add-on per MSSP — meaningful without requiring procurement approval.

The Proof-of-Value Loop

1
Free AI Agent Risk Report (discovery wedge) Deliver a one-time risk scorecard for one client tenant — no contract, no friction. The vCISO sees what they don't know.
2
Urgency created by the score "Your AI agent risk score is 7.2/10 across 12 clients." That number creates a buying trigger — the vCISO has to act or document why they didn't.
3
Deploy SecureLayer → score improves month over month Office Hours enforcement, Skill Registry, and Policy Engine drive the score down over 30–90 days.
4
Score = renewal conversation + client upsell justification "Score dropped from 7.2 to 2.1 since deployment." The vCISO shows this to their clients and their own management. SecureLayer becomes a retained asset, not a discretionary spend.

Pricing Sensitivity

Key variable: compliance driver

If AI agent governance appears in a compliance framework (SOC 2, EU AI Act, NIST AI RMF), pricing power shifts significantly:

Discretionary monitoring add-on → $20–30/tenant/month
Compliance-required control → $50–75/tenant/month

This is a 2–3× revenue difference. Validate the compliance angle in discovery calls by asking whether AI agent access controls have come up in any recent audit.

Validation Gate — Before Any Sales Investment

Critical Gap

The business model is currently built on inferred demand and adjacent intuition. No MSSP vCISO has confirmed pain, expressed urgency, or indicated willingness to pay. The three discovery calls below are the minimum required before any pricing, packaging, or sales motion is committed to.

Discovery Call Protocol

Book 3 calls with MSSP vCISOs through existing relationships. Do not pitch SecureLayer. Do not mention the product. Ask them to walk through how they handle a client security incident involving a developer tool. Listen for whether AI agents come up unprompted.

The Single Most Important Question to Ask

"Walk me through what happened the last time a client had a security incident involving a developer tool or AI assistant."

If AI agents come up unprompted → demand is real.
If you have to introduce the concept → awareness problem; education-led GTM needed first.
If they describe a workaround you didn't know existed → real competitor; adjust the model.

Action Items

  • This week Identify 3 MSSP vCISO contacts through existing adjacent relationships. Book 45-minute discovery calls — no deck, no demo.
  • This week In each call, ask the standard incident walkthrough question. Record whether AI agents come up unprompted. Note exact language they use to describe the problem (or absence of it).
  • After calls Based on findings, determine pricing tier: compliance-driven ($50–75/tenant/month) or proactive monitoring ($20–30/tenant/month).
  • After calls Ask one call participant to accept a free AI Agent Risk Report for one of their clients. Deliver it manually (even as a formatted PDF). Observe their reaction — that is the real wedge test.
  • If validated Approach ConnectWise Invent or N-able TAP for channel partnership. MSSP-channel distribution removes the need for a direct salesforce and puts SecureLayer in front of thousands of vCISOs.
  • If validated Talk to one SOC 2 auditor. Ask if AI agent access controls have appeared in any recent client audit. If yes, the compliance wedge is real — reprice accordingly.
