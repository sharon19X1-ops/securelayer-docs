The four questions — what to say
Q1 · Is it a real, deployed product?
They're really asking: can I trust this is real, or is it a deck?
Say this

"Yes — there's a working product you can click through today, and I'll send you the link. The architecture is a complete engineering spec: RMM deployment, PSA integration (ConnectWise, Autotask, NinjaOne), SIEM output (Splunk, Sentinel, Elastic), and the skill-scoring engine."

"We're in the Phase 1 build and taking on a small number of design partners to develop it around real MSSP environments. That's exactly why I wanted to talk to you — I'd like yours to be one of them."

Q2 · Beyond monitoring filesystem & network?
They're really asking: how is this different from the EDR I already run?
Say this

"Traditional EDR sees that a process ran and what file it touched. SecureLayer works one level up — at the semantic layer, between the AI agent and the action it's about to take."

"We intercept at the IDE plugin and the MCP server interface, so we see the intent — the skill being invoked, the tool call, the permission requested — and apply a policy decision before the action runs. That's skill-level enforcement, office-hours enforcement, and MCP interception. Not just watching what happened — governing what's allowed to happen."

Q3 · The skills detection capability
They're really asking: what exactly is a "skill" and how does scoring work?
Say this

"A 'skill' is a discrete capability an AI agent can invoke — browser-fetch, git-push, env-reader, shell-exec-sudo. We keep a live registry of every skill across all your tenants and score each one across 10 risk dimensions: permission scope, network egress, credential access, shell execution, prompt-injection susceptibility, and so on."

"The policy is automatic. Score 8.0+ is critical — you choose: auto-block, or hold it for your decision. 3.0 and under is auto-approved, so your analysts never touch autocomplete or routine git pushes. Everything in between routes to your review queue with the full score breakdown and the MITRE technique. Your team only ever looks at the middle band."

10-dimension scoreauto-approve ≤ 3.0review 3.1–7.9critical ≥ 8.0 → block or holdMITRE-tagged
Q4 · RMM leverage · self-protection · false positives
They're really asking: can you deploy at my scale without breaking my clients?
Say this

On RMM — yes, you guessed right. "Deployment is native to the RMM you already run — NinjaOne, Datto, N-able, Kaseya, ConnectWise. One script, thousands of endpoints, installed as a system service, with hook health visible in your RMM console."

On self-protection. "Phase 1 has no endpoint agent at all — discovery is cloud-side, so there's nothing on the endpoint to compromise, and a compromised host can't forge an all-clear to us. In Phase 2 the runtime component is code-signed, runs under its own service account, and if it's ever silenced it shows up as an offline alert in your RMM. Silence is a visible alert, not an invisible bypass."

On false positives — this is the one we engineered hardest. "We're conservative by default: auto-block only at 8.0+. For critical actions you can set a human-in-the-loop hold instead of a block — the action pauses and you get a notification to approve or deny. And any blocked developer has one-click break-glass straight to your queue. You tune the threshold per client. The whole point is to enforce without generating angry client calls."
